## IA recap
Real power $[\,\pu{ W }]$
$$
P=\operatorname{Re}\left[ \tilde{V}\tilde{I}^{*} \right] =VI\cos \varphi
$$
It is dissipated by resistors and $\cos \varphi$ is known as the *power factor*

Reactive Power $[\,\pu{ VARs }]$
$$
Q=\operatorname{Im}\left[ \tilde{V}\tilde{I}^{*} \right] =VI\sin \varphi
$$
It is consumed by inductors and generated by capacitors. Inductive loads (positive impedance) *lag* in $\varphi$ while capacitive loads (negative impedance) *lead*.
$$
\begin{split}
Q_{L}&=I^{2}X_{L}=\frac{V_{L}^{2}}{X_{L}}\\
Q_{C}&=-I^{2}X_{C}=-\frac{V_{L}^{2}}{X_{C}}
\end{split}
$$
$P$ and $Q$ are both conserved.

Apparent power $[\pu{\, VA }]$
$$
\begin{split}
\mathbf{S}&=P+\mathbf{j}Q\\
|S|&=VI=\sqrt{ P^{2}+Q^{2} }
\end{split}
$$

## Star-connected three-phase supply

![[star-connected-circuit.png|500]]
A balanced three-phase supply is produced by three voltages of equal magnitude differing in phase by 120°.
![[three-phase-supply.png|300]]
The *star point* is the central point of the supply and is kept at a neutral potential.

The voltages of across the supplies relative to the star-point are known as *phase voltages*.

### Line voltages
![[line-voltages.png|500]]
$$
\begin{split}
|\tilde{V}_{AB}|&=2|\tilde{V}_{A}|\sin 60^{\circ}=\sqrt{ 3 }V\\ \\
 \arg{\tilde{V}_{AB}}&=\arg{\tilde{V}}_{A}+30^{\circ}=30^{\circ}
\end{split}
$$
Hence,
$$\text{line voltage}=\sqrt{ 3 }\times\text{phase voltage}$$

>In modern electric grids with a $\pu{ 240V}$ power supply, the corresponding line voltage is $\sim \pu{ 415V }$

So with a balanced star-load ($Z_{A}=Z_{B}=Z_{C}$), the balanced supply will produce balanced currents.
![[star-currents.png|400]]
Note that
$$
\text{line current}=\text{phase current}
$$
Once again, the star-point of the three-phase load is at a neutral current and voltage.

> If both the supply and load are balanced, the neutral conductor is redundant and carries no current.

## Three-phase real and reactive power
For a balanced 3-phase load, $P$, $Q$ and $S$ are identical in each phase. This leads to
$$
\begin{cases}
\begin{align}
P_{T} & =3P_{i} \\
Q_{T} & =3Q_{i} \\
\mathbf{S}_{T} & =3\mathbf{S}_{i}
\end{align}
\end{cases}
$$
Since each phase behaves *identically*, the analysis can be simplified to just one of them.
![[single-phase-equivalent.png|500]]

## Delta-connected loads
![[delta-connected-laod.png|500]]
The voltage across each phase of the delta-connected load equals the line voltage:
$$
\begin{align}
\tilde{V}_{1}&=\tilde{V}_{AB} \\
\tilde{V}_{2} & =\tilde{V}_{BC} \\
\tilde{V}_{3} & =\tilde{V}_{CA}
\end{align}
$$
And the load currents form a balanced 3-phase set.
![[3-phase-balanced-load-current.png|500]]
By Kirchhoff’s Current Law, e.g. for line A:
$$
\tilde{I}_{A}=\tilde{I}_{1}-\tilde{I}_{3}
$$
So, by trigonometry,
$$
|\tilde{I}_{A}|=\sqrt{ 3 }|\tilde{I}_{1}|
$$
## Summary for star and delta connections
| Phase quantity | Star $\star$ | Delta $\Delta$  |
| ---- | ---- | ---- |
| Voltage  | $V_{\mathrm{line}} /\sqrt{3 }$ | $V_{\mathrm{line}}$ |
| Current  | $I_{\mathrm{line}}$ | $I_{\mathrm{line}} /\sqrt{ 3 }$ |
## $\Delta$-connected supply
Because the supply phase voltages are a balanced three-phase set that sum to 0, they can theoretically be connected as $\Delta$.
![[delta-supply.png|400]]
However, this is not typical because any small imbalance between the phase voltages would drive a circulating current limited only by the internal resistance.

## $\star-\Delta$ transformation
![[star-delta-transform.png|500]]
As in the Databook:
$$
\bar{Z}_{ab}= \frac{\sum \bar{Z}_{i}\bar{Z}_{j}}{\bar{Z}_{c}}
$$
But for balanced loads:
$$
\begin{align}&
\begin{cases}
\begin{aligned}
\bar{Z}_{\star}&=\bar{Z}_{a}=\bar{Z}_{b}=\bar{Z}_{c} \\
\bar{Z}_{\Delta} & =\bar{Z}_{ab}=\bar{Z}_{bc}=\bar{Z}_{ca} \\
\end{aligned}
\end{cases}
\\
 \\
&\Rightarrow\quad \bar{Z}_{ab} =\bar{Z}_{\Delta}=3\bar{Z}_{\star} \\

\end{align}
$$

## Total 3-phase $P$, $Q$, and $\mathbf{S}$
$$
P=3V_{\mathrm{phase}}I_\mathrm{phase}\cos \varphi
$$
If the load (or source) is star-connected:
$$
\begin{align}
V_{\mathrm{phase}} & =\frac{V_{\mathrm{\ell}}}{\sqrt{ 3 }} \\
I_{\mathrm{phase}} & =I_{\mathrm{\ell}} \\
 \\
\Rightarrow\quad P & =\sqrt{ 3 }V_{\mathrm{\ell}}I_{\ell}\cos \varphi
\end{align}
$$

If the load (or source) is delta-connected:
$$
\begin{align}
V_{\mathrm{phase}} & =V_{\mathrm{\ell}} \\
I_{\mathrm{phase}} & =\frac{I_{\mathrm{\ell}}}{\sqrt{ 3 }} \\
 \\
\Rightarrow\quad P & =\sqrt{ 3 }V_{\mathrm{\ell}}I_{\ell}\cos \varphi
\end{align}
$$
So in terms of line quantities, when either the load or source is $\star$ or $\Delta$ connected,
$$
\begin{align}
P & =\sqrt{ 3 }V_{\ell}I_{\ell}\cos \varphi \\
Q & =\sqrt{ 3 }V_{\ell}I_{\ell}\sin \varphi \\
|\mathbf{S}| & =\sqrt{ 3 }V_{\ell}I_{\ell}
\end{align}
$$
